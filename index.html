Work About Cherrie Kwok

What I do . . .

I am a doctoral candidate at the University of Virginia and an Associate Editor at Undisciplining the Victorian Classroom.

Broadly speaking, I focus on global Anglophone literature from the long nineteenth and early twentieth-centuries, with interests in an artistic style called Decadence and its relationship to Empire and postcolonial theory. My dissertation, which has been supported by the Jefferson Scholars Foundation since 2020, examines how African-American, Caribbean, East-Asian, South-Asian, and Indigenous writers in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries produced new versions of Decadence in order to articulate anti-imperial perspectives in their literature. In the process, I expose how Decadence—commonly thought of as an imperialist aesthetic—can support anti-imperial politics. Some of my early work on these topics recently received the 2021 British Association for Decadence Studies Postgraduate Essay prize.

I also have interests in the digital humanities, where my work has been supported by the UVa School of Data Science and the Alfred P. Sloan Foundation.

In 2022, I received the Distinguished Graduate Teaching Award in the Arts and Humanities for my teaching and the Predoctoral-Postdoctoral Jefferson Fellowship for my research. From 2020-2022, I held the Elizabeth Arendall Tilney and Schuyler Merritt Tilney Jefferson Fellowship. I completed my MA at New York University, where I received the Millicent Bell Prize for the most outstanding Master's thesis in the English Department.

I am co-chairing a university-wide symposium about anti-racist teaching at UVa this November 2022. I will also chair a conference about Decadence in March 2023, titled "Global Decadence, Race, and the Futures of Decadence Studies." Please reach out if you are interested in participating in either of these events.
